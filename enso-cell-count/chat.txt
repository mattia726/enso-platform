Bottom line

The accurate strategy is not to train cell quantity from ABSOLUTE purity alone. Purity is a fraction; cellularity is an amount/density. The de-risked path is to build a second model, EnsoCellularity, that predicts nucleated-cell / nuclei count per tile from the same Virchow tile embeddings, trained using nuclear instance-segmentation labels generated from TCGA images and validated against public/manual nuclei datasets.

Then, at inference:

estimated cells selected by doctor
= sum(predicted total nuclei per selected tile)

estimated tumor cells selected
= sum(predicted total nuclei per tile × local tumor fraction from EnsoPurity)

This gives the doctor exactly what you want: orders of magnitude of total cells and tumor cells in the selected region, not a fake exact number.

Your existing TCGA/GDC pipeline is already an excellent substrate: it has GDC same-portion ABSOLUTE matching, normals assigned purity 0.0, Virchow tile embeddings, tile coordinates, and pathologist percent_tumor_nuclei availability for a large fraction of frozen-section slides .

1. What TCGA/GDC can give us for this task

GDC represents biospecimens hierarchically: a case has samples; samples have portions; portions generate slides and analytes; analytes generate aliquots used for sequencing. This is exactly why your same-portion GDC API matching is the correct biological linkage for purity/ploidy labels. GDC explicitly describes biospecimen data as hierarchical and notes that portions are used to generate diagnostic slides while aliquots are associated with sequencing data.

The TCGA barcode documentation also confirms the relevant structure: samples are split into vials and portions, analytes are extracted from portions, and tissue slides can be TS, BS, or MS, followed by a number or letter indicating slide order. This supports your conclusion that same-portion matching should come from the GDC biospecimen tree, not naïve string slicing of the WSI barcode.

For this new model, the useful TCGA/GDC fields are:

Data source	Level	Use for cell-count model
Raw H&E WSI pixels	tile / slide	Needed to generate nuclear segmentation labels.
Virchow tile embeddings	tile	Input to the cheap second model.
Tile coordinates	tile	Required to map labels and sum selected ROIs.
MPP / dimensions	slide	Required to normalize counts by physical area.
ABSOLUTE purity	aliquot	Use for tumor fraction model and validation, not total cell count.
ABSOLUTE ploidy	aliquot	Optional DNA-mass / genome-equivalent estimate.
GDC percent_tumor_nuclei	slide	Validation/auxiliary tumor-fraction signal, not total cell count.
GDC percent_tumor_cells / percent_stromal_cells	slide	Coarse validation of composition, when available.
sample type 11 normal	slide	Keep as purity 0.0 controls, but not as low-cell controls.

GDC lists percent stromal cells, percent tumor cells, and percent tumor nuclei as optional slide-level fields, but these are percentages, not counts. They can help validate composition, but they cannot tell us whether a tile has 5, 50, or 250 nuclei.

ABSOLUTE is valuable because it infers tumor purity and malignant-cell ploidy from somatic DNA alterations; the PanCanAtlas page exposes the TCGA_mastercalls.abs_tables_JSedit.fixed.txt purity/ploidy file you are already using.

2. What TCGA/GDC does not give us

TCGA/GDC does not provide a reliable official field like:

tile_id → total nuclei count
tile_id → total viable cells
tile_id → tumor nuclei count
tile_id → stromal nuclei count
tile_id → lymphocyte count

So the cell-count label must come from image-derived nuclei segmentation, not from purity.

This is the key modeling separation:

purity / tumor content = fraction of tumor cells
cellularity / cell amount = number of cells or nuclei
tumor-cell amount = fraction × number

A slide can have:

high purity + low cellularity
low purity + high cellularity
high purity + high cellularity
normal tissue + high cellularity
necrotic tissue + near-zero viable cellularity

Therefore, EnsoPurity and EnsoCellularity should be separate models that are combined downstream.

3. Best public data source found: TCGA pan-cancer nuclei segmentations

The strongest directly relevant public resource is Pan-Cancer-Nuclei-Seg, a TCGA nuclei segmentation dataset. The Scientific Data paper reports automatic nucleus segmentation on 5,060 TCGA whole-slide images across 10 TCGA cancer types, representing roughly 5 billion segmented objects, plus manual segmentation labels on 1,356 patches across 14 cancer types. It also reports WSI-level and patch-level quality control; after comparison to manual segmentation, the released segmentation data had average Dice of at least 77% and average instance-level Dice of at least 62% in every released cancer type.

This is extremely important because it gives us a real-world, TCGA-native source of nuclei counts. The DICOM-converted release stores nuclei annotations as closed polygons with area, and provides GCS/AWS manifests for programmatic download; the metadata lists TCGA collections including BLCA, BRCA, CESC, COAD, GBM, LUAD, LUSC, PAAD, PRAD, READ, SKCM, STAD, UCEC, and UVM.

However, this dataset may not fully overlap with your current frozen-section TS/MS/BS wedge dataset, because many TCGA public nuclei resources include diagnostic DX slides. The correct first step is therefore an overlap audit:

Pan-Cancer-Nuclei-Seg barcode/file_id
∩
your wedge_mvp_dataset.xlsx file_uuid_original/barcode

If there is overlap, those slides become direct high-quality count supervision. If overlap is mostly DX, the dataset is still extremely valuable for validating the segmentation teacher and coordinate-counting pipeline, but not sufficient alone for final training on your frozen-section distribution.

4. Other useful public nuclei datasets and models

These are the datasets/models I would use to de-risk the teacher segmentation layer.

Resource	Why it matters
Pan-Cancer-Nuclei-Seg	TCGA-native WSI-scale nuclei segmentation; best direct evidence source.
PanNuke	Pan-cancer H&E nuclei instance segmentation/classification with clinically useful classes.
MoNuSeg	Multi-organ TCGA-derived nuclei segmentation benchmark.
NuCLS	TCGA breast cancer nuclei annotations with pathologist/non-pathologist/multirater data.
CryoNuSeg	Frozen-section H&E nuclei segmentation; important because your wedge dataset is frozen TS/MS/BS.
HoVer-Net	Strong nuclei instance segmentation/classification architecture for H&E.
CellViT	Modern ViT-based nuclei segmentation/classification trained/evaluated on PanNuke.
StarDist / Cellpose	Useful independent teacher baselines and disagreement checks.
OpenTME	New TCGA cell-level/TME profile resource; useful as external sanity check, not primary labels yet.

PanNuke contains about 200k nuclei in five clinically important classes and 19 tissue types, with clinical-pathologist QC and “clinical wild” diversity. This is useful for class-specific nuclei counts: neoplastic, inflammatory, connective, epithelial, dead/non-viable.

NuCLS contains more than 220k labeled nuclei from TCGA breast cancer images, with both single-rater and multi-rater annotations; the GigaScience paper reports 128k nucleus annotations from 3,944 fields of view and 125 triple-negative breast cancer patients, plus about 97.3k multi-rater annotations. This is useful for measuring annotation variability and calibration.

CryoNuSeg is especially relevant because it is fully annotated H&E frozen-section nuclei data and explicitly notes that frozen-section preparation can degrade image quality and change nuclear appearance relative to FFPE. That directly de-risks your use case because your TCGA wedge uses frozen TS/MS/BS sections.

HoVer-Net was designed for simultaneous segmentation and classification of nuclei in H&E images, including clustered/overlapping nuclei, and introduced a colorectal H&E dataset with 24,319 exhaustively annotated nuclei.

StarDist is an independent nuclei/cell detection method based on star-convex polygons, useful as a second teacher or disagreement detector.

Cellpose is a generalist segmentation method for cells and nuclei across varied microscopy images; it is useful as a robustness baseline, although not pathology-specific enough to be the only teacher.

CellViT is a more modern pathology-specific option: it performs automated instance segmentation of cell nuclei using a Vision Transformer and was trained/evaluated on PanNuke.

OpenTME is a very recent TCGA resource: it provides precomputed tumor microenvironment profiles for 3,634 TCGA H&E WSIs across five cancer types and includes tissue QC, tissue segmentation, cell detection/classification, and more than 4,500 quantitative readouts per slide. Because it is new and model-derived, I would use it as an external sanity check rather than as the primary training source.

5. Correct model target: “nucleated-cell estimate,” not literal full-cell count

On H&E, the reliable visible object is the nucleus. Cell membranes and full cytoplasmic boundaries are often not reliably visible in solid-tumor H&E. So the product should internally predict:

nuclei_count_per_tile

and externally present it as:

estimated nucleated cells

This is clinically honest and useful for NGS adequacy. It also avoids false precision.

For each 224×224 tile at 0.5 µm/pixel:

tile side = 224 × 0.5 µm = 112 µm
tile area = 112 × 112 µm² = 12,544 µm²
tile area = 0.012544 mm²

So the model should output both:

nuclei_per_tile
nuclei_per_mm²

For example:

100 nuclei in one tile ≈ 7,973 nuclei/mm²

This normalization is important if any slide has imperfect MPP metadata.

6. Recommended architecture

Do not use a slide-level MIL model for this second task at first. Cell count is naturally tile-level.

Use a cheap tile-level student model:

Virchow tile embedding
+ optional 3×3 local-neighborhood embedding summary
+ tissue fraction / MPP metadata
→ EnsoCellularity head
→ predicted total nuclei count + uncertainty

Virchow is a strong choice for the feature substrate because it is a 632M-parameter pathology foundation model trained with DINOv2 on 1.5M H&E WSIs from diverse tissues/specimens, with strong tile-level and slide-level benchmark performance.

Recommended output head:

μ_count              # expected nuclei count
dispersion           # for negative-binomial uncertainty
count_bin            # ordinal bin for UI
q05, q50, q95         # optional quantile interval
quality_flag         # background / low-confidence / artifact

Recommended losses:

L = NegativeBinomialLoss(count)
  + MAE(log1p(count))
  + ordinal count-bin cross entropy
  + quantile loss for prediction interval calibration

Why not plain MSE? Counts are non-negative, skewed, and heteroscedastic. A 20-cell error is huge for a nearly empty tile and small for a 300-cell tile.

Useful UI bins:

0 / background
1–10 nuclei
10–50 nuclei
50–150 nuclei
150–300 nuclei
>300 nuclei

For the doctor, show an aggregated order-of-magnitude estimate, not per-tile exactness.

7. Label-generation strategy
Step A — Try direct TCGA nuclei-label overlap

First join your wedge slide table to Pan-Cancer-Nuclei-Seg:

join key candidates:
- exact barcode
- normalized TCGA slide submitter_id
- file_uuid / GDC file_id where available
- case/sample/vial/slide suffix as fallback only

For overlapping slides:

nucleus polygon / centroid → tile coordinate → nuclei count per tile

Use a centroid rule:

count nucleus if nucleus centroid lies inside the tile

This avoids double-counting border nuclei.

Better still, when computing labels from raw segmentation, extract a halo:

central tile: 224×224
segmentation crop: 320×320 or 384×384
count only centroids inside central 224×224

This prevents border truncation from undercounting nuclei.

Step B — Generate labels on your actual frozen-section wedge slides

Because your production model should work on your TS/MS/BS frozen-section distribution, generate pseudo-labels directly on the raw tiles from your wedge slides:

raw TCGA WSI tile
→ teacher nuclei segmentation
→ total nuclei count
→ optional nuclei class counts
→ label attached to Virchow embedding row

Teacher ensemble:

Teacher 1: CellViT or HoVer-Net trained/fine-tuned on PanNuke
Teacher 2: StarDist histology or Cellpose nuclei
Teacher 3: optional TCGA Pan-Cancer-Nuclei-Seg calibration model

Use teacher disagreement as uncertainty:

if |teacher_A_count - teacher_B_count| is high:
    downweight or route to manual audit
Step C — Human audit set

A small audited set is mandatory before claiming clinical utility.

Recommended audit size:

3,000–5,000 tiles

Stratify by:

TCGA project / organ
sample type: 01, 02, 05, 06, 07, 11
section type: TS, MS, BS
count bin
purity bin
percent_tumor_nuclei bin
frozen artifacts
necrosis
lymphocyte-rich regions
stroma-rich regions
background
scanner/stain variation

The annotation task can be point-counting nuclei, not full mask tracing. Full masks are useful for a subset, but for your product, count calibration is more important than perfect contours.

8. How to use purity and pathologist fields correctly
ABSOLUTE purity

Use it for EnsoPurity and for tumor-cell estimation:

predicted_tumor_nuclei_tile
= predicted_total_nuclei_tile × local_tumor_fraction_tile

Do not use ABSOLUTE purity as a label for total cells.

ABSOLUTE ploidy

Use it only for optional DNA/genome-equivalent estimates:

DNA_pg_tile
≈ nuclei_count_tile × 6.46 × [(1 - f_tumor) + f_tumor × (ploidy / 2)]

A human diploid cell is commonly estimated at about 6.46 pg DNA.

GDC percent_tumor_nuclei

Use it as a slide-level validation signal:

slide_predicted_tumor_nuclei_fraction
= sum(predicted_tumor_nuclei_tile) / sum(predicted_total_nuclei_tile)

Compare that with:

GDC percent_tumor_nuclei / 100

This validates the composition side. It does not validate total count.

Normal slides

Sample type 11 should be:

purity = 0.0
tumor_nuclei ≈ 0
total_nuclei may be high

The GDC sample-type table defines code 11 as Solid Tissue Normal, while codes 01, 02, 05, 06, and 07 cover primary, recurrent, new primary, metastatic, and additional metastatic tumor samples.

9. Inference-time product behavior

For a doctor-selected region R:

estimated_total_nucleated_cells(R)
= Σ_i coverage_i × predicted_total_nuclei_i

where coverage_i handles partially selected tiles.

Tumor-cell estimate:

estimated_tumor_nuclei(R)
= Σ_i coverage_i × predicted_total_nuclei_i × predicted_tumor_fraction_i

Optional DNA estimate:

estimated_DNA_ng(R)
≈ Σ_i DNA_pg_i / 1000

UI example:

Selected region: 6.2 mm²
Estimated nucleated cells: ~85k
Likely range: 45k–140k
Estimated tumor nuclei: ~52k
Estimated tumor fraction: ~61%
Approximate pre-extraction DNA: ~0.5 µg
Confidence: medium

The UI should never imply exact extraction yield. Extraction yield depends on fixation, necrosis, tissue processing, scraping efficiency, extraction chemistry, and lab protocol.

12. Major risks and mitigations
Risk	Why it matters	Mitigation
Purity confused with count	Produces biologically wrong labels	Separate EnsoPurity and EnsoCellularity.
Public nuclei labels mostly DX/FFPE	Your wedge is TS/MS/BS frozen	Use public data for teacher validation; generate labels on actual wedge frozen slides.
Frozen artifacts	Nuclei appearance changes in FS	Include CryoNuSeg and TCGA FS audit tiles.
Teacher bias	Student inherits wrong labels	Teacher ensemble, disagreement filtering, human audit calibration.
Border undercount	Nuclei cut by tile edges	Use halo extraction and centroid-in-central-tile rule.
MPP errors	Counts depend on physical area	Predict both per tile and per mm²; verify MPP.
Necrosis/mucin/background	Tissue area may contain few viable cells	Add QC/artifact head and count-bin model.
Tumor vs non-tumor confusion	Doctor needs tumor-cell amount too	Combine total count with EnsoPurity; optionally train class-specific nuclei counts.
Slide leakage	Inflated validation	Split by case/aliquot, never by tile.
Clinical overclaim	“Exact cells” and “DNA yield” are unsafe	Present order-of-magnitude ranges and confidence.
13. Final recommended design

Build:

EnsoPurity:
    Virchow tile embeddings → tumor fraction / purity heatmap

EnsoCellularity:
    Virchow tile embeddings → total nuclei count / cellularity heatmap

Derived:
    tumor nuclei per tile = total nuclei × tumor fraction

This is the most evidence-based and de-risked design because it respects the biology:

purity = composition
cellularity = quantity
NGS adequacy = quantity × composition × tissue quality

The first clinical/product claim should be:

“Estimated nucleated-cell quantity and tumor-cell quantity in the selected region, reported as an order-of-magnitude adequacy aid for molecular testing.”

Not:

“Exact cell count” or “guaranteed DNA yield.”

The strongest immediate next technical step is the overlap audit between your wedge slides and Pan-Cancer-Nuclei-Seg, followed by a teacher-segmentation run on your actual TS/MS/BS frozen-section tiles and a small human-count audit set. That gives you a defensible label source, a cheap Virchow-based student model, and a validation path that should stand up clinically.
